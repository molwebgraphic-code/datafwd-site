From Driving Lessons to Data Science: The Story Behind DataFWD

Moshood Lawal · Apr 3, 2026 · 5 min read
ML
Moshood Lawal
Founder, DataFWD · BSc Computer Science · MSc Data Science

I didn't set out to build a data science community. I set out to pass my UK driving test.

This is the story of how those two things became the same problem — and why I built DataFWD to solve both of them.

The driving test problem

Learning to drive in the UK is harder than most people expect. The practical test has a first-attempt pass rate of under 50%. That means more than half of all learners fail — not because their instructors are bad, but because of a structural problem that nobody had solved.

Every driving lesson is delivered verbally, in real-time, in a moving vehicle. Your instructor gives you feedback across multiple skills simultaneously — clutch control, mirror checks, road positioning, hazard awareness. You can't take notes. You're driving. And by the time you get home, most of that feedback has faded.

I experienced this myself. I was having lessons, making progress, then arriving at the next lesson having half-forgotten what we'd covered. Progress felt circular. The instruction was good. The retention was the problem.

What I built to fix it

I'm a data scientist by training. When I see a structured problem, I want to build a structured solution.

So I started recording my lessons with my instructor's consent, transcribing the audio after each session, and mapping what was said to the 27 official DVSA competency areas that examiners assess on the test. After each lesson I'd review a structured summary of exactly what we'd covered, which skills needed more work, and what to focus on before the next session.

Across more than 20 real driving lessons, I built and refined this system manually. Then I sat the UK practical driving test — and passed first time.

The system worked. The problem was that it was entirely manual — hours of work after each lesson, just to extract what any learner should be able to see automatically.

That became Navly: a mobile application that automates the entire workflow. Record the lesson. Get a structured competency report. Know exactly what to work on before the next session. But that's a story for another day.

The same problem in data science

While I was building this, I kept noticing a parallel problem in my own data science development.

I have a BSc in Computer Science and an MSc in Data Science. I've spent a decade in performance marketing, running six-figure campaigns for education and tech companies. I've co-founded an EdTech company. On paper, the skills are there.

But the thing I kept running into — the thing that held me back more than any technical gap — was the absence of a feedback loop.

I'd build a project. I'd think it was solid. I'd share it. And then someone would ask a question I hadn't considered, point out an assumption I'd made without realising, or suggest an approach I hadn't known existed. Every time that happened, the project got significantly better. But those conversations were rare and accidental. There was no structure to make them happen consistently.

The data science community online is enormous. Discord servers, Slack groups, LinkedIn networks, Reddit threads. But none of them were designed around the thing that actually makes you better: working on real projects with people who will give you honest, specific feedback and hold you accountable for shipping.

Why DataFWD

DataFWD is built around a simple belief: data science is a team sport, and most people are practising it alone.

The best data teams in the world — at Google, at the NHS, at early-stage startups — don't work in isolation. Code gets reviewed. Assumptions get challenged. Findings get stress-tested. The lone genius is a myth. The productive analyst is one who gets regular, structured feedback from peers who understand what they're building.

DataFWD creates that environment for data scientists who don't have it at work yet — or who want more of it outside work. Peer-led squads. Real projects. Code review. Honest feedback. Accountability that makes you actually ship.

What I've learned building both

Building Navly and DataFWD simultaneously has taught me the same lesson in two different contexts: the gap between learning and progressing is almost always a feedback problem, not a knowledge problem.

Driving learners don't fail because they haven't been taught. They fail because the instruction they received isn't structured in a way they can act on between lessons.

Data scientists don't stall because they haven't done enough courses. They stall because nobody is reviewing their work, questioning their assumptions, or making them articulate their reasoning out loud.

Fix the feedback loop, and progress follows.

DataFWD is early. There's a lot still to build. But the mission is clear: give every data scientist access to the kind of collaborative, accountable environment that the best practitioners already have — regardless of where they work or where they are in their career.

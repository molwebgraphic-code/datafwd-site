Meet the Founder: Moshood Lawal on Data Science, Community, and Building DataFWD

Moshood Lawal · Apr 3, 2026 · 5 min read
ML
Moshood Lawal
Founder, DataFWD · BSc Computer Science · MSc Data Science

I've spent the last decade sitting at the intersection of data, marketing, and technology — running campaigns, co-founding companies, and trying to understand why some people advance quickly in data science while others plateau. DataFWD is my answer to that question.

Here's the longer version of the story.

The academic foundation

I studied Computer Science at undergraduate level, which gave me the fundamentals — algorithms, systems, software engineering thinking. But it was my Master's in Data Science that changed how I see the world.

The MSc wasn't just about learning new tools. It was about developing a different kind of reasoning: how to ask better questions of data, how to design analyses that actually answer those questions, and how to be honest about what the data can and cannot tell you. That shift in thinking — from "I know Python" to "I know how to think with data" — is the thing most data science courses don't explicitly teach.

The gap between a data scientist who can run models and one who can drive decisions isn't technical. It's about judgement, communication, and the ability to know when your analysis is wrong.

Ten years in performance marketing

Before and alongside my academic work, I spent a decade in performance marketing — running six-figure campaigns for companies in the education and technology sectors. This wasn't a detour from data science. It was one of the most intensive data science educations I've ever had.

Performance marketing is applied statistics under commercial pressure. Every decision — which audience to target, which creative to run, how much to bid, when to scale — is a hypothesis that the data either validates or kills. You learn very quickly to distinguish signal from noise, to avoid optimising for the wrong metric, and to present findings in a way that makes someone act, not just nod.

After ten years of that, I have a very specific view of what makes a data scientist genuinely valuable: not the algorithms they know, but the business questions they can formulate and the clarity with which they can communicate answers.

Co-founding in EdTech

I co-founded an EdTech company focused on AI, data analysis, and cybersecurity education. Building that company taught me things no academic programme does: how to talk to users before you build, how to design learning experiences that actually change behaviour, and how to run a team around a mission rather than just a product.

It also gave me a front-row seat to a problem I kept seeing across every learning context I worked in. People were acquiring knowledge — taking courses, earning credentials, building individual skills — but they weren't advancing. The missing ingredient, almost universally, was structured feedback from peers who understood what they were building.

The gap DataFWD fills

The data science community online is enormous and genuinely generous. But most of it is optimised for consumption, not for output. You can watch thousands of hours of tutorials, join hundreds of Discord servers, and collect certificates indefinitely — and still not have built anything you're genuinely proud of, because nobody has pushed back on your thinking or held you accountable for shipping.

What the best data scientists I know have in common isn't a specific tool or a particular algorithm. It's that they've had their work seriously reviewed by people who understood it and were honest about where it was weak. They've had to explain their reasoning out loud to someone who asked "why?" three times. They've built in a context where someone else was waiting for their work.

That environment produces better data scientists faster than any course. And it's the environment most people don't have access to — unless they happen to work at a company with a strong data culture, or know the right people.

DataFWD is built to change that. Peer-led squads, structured around real projects, with code review, pair analysis, and genuine accountability. Not a chat room. A team.

Why now

Data science is maturing as a field. The tools are commoditising. The basic skills — Python, SQL, machine learning fundamentals — are table stakes, not differentiators. What separates the data scientists who get hired, who get promoted, who lead teams, is increasingly the things that can't be learned from a course: communication, judgement, the ability to work effectively with others, and a portfolio of work that proves it.

The window for building a community that genuinely helps people develop those skills — rather than just selling them another credential — is open right now. That's why DataFWD exists, and why I'm building it.

If you're a data scientist who wants to build better, ship more, and develop with a squad behind you — DataFWD is for you. Come and build with us.
